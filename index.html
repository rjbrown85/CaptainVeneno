Artist Bio

The full dose

“It's like Tool and Joy Division got in a bar fight and nobody won.”— guy who came for the other band

“They're loud. They're gloomy. But at least the amps are in tune.”— somebody's dad

“A bass tone should not be allowed to make me feel things.”— spiritually rearranged patron

We all have a curfew. Ours is the noise ordinance.

From the ashes of Shanghai's lockdown rose the most rocking, rip-roaring, flat-out amazing band of all time. Four people, one practice room with terrible ventilation, and a shared conviction that the city owed them a few thousand watts of catharsis. Captain Veneno is what happens when boredom, big amps, and questionable life choices are left unsupervised for two years.

Their influences run deep and run wide: the swagger of Queens of the Stone Age, the menace of early Soundgarden, the art-school gloom of a certain moody five-piece from Oxford — and look, you're already thinking it, you've half-said the name in your head, but no. Not them. Adjacent to them, in the next building over from them, on nodding terms with them at the bus stop. Captain Veneno took that paranoid-android atmosphere, fed it through a fuzz pedal, and drove it straight into a wall of stoner-rock riffs instead.

Throw in the desert grooves of Kyuss, a dash of Deftones tenderness, the occasional Fugazi elbow to the ribs, and the unhinged joy of a Turbonegro encore, and you've got a sound that is loud, brooding, danceable, and legally distinct from anyone you might be tempted to name.

Some nights the set gets a little experimental — ok, computer, that came out wrong — what we mean is Josh kicks on a fuzz pedal, the whole thing tips toward the weird, and then a riff drags us back to being a rock band again.

People say the mood runs a touch paranoid — android, even — and sure, fine — but there's a four-on-the-floor stomp under all that gloom, so nobody needs to lie down in a dark room about it.

Is it art rock? Is it a slow creep — no, not that — toward a chorus that finally, mercifully, hits? Yes — that one — every single time.

(Yes, we used em dashes — lots of them — em dashes existed long before any chatbot did, so this is just us. Don't accuse us of anything.)

Art & Merch

Nothing for sale

In an age where a machine can generate a thousand album covers before your coffee gets cold, what is the point of making art at all? The market is flooded, the muse is automated, and originality is just a prompt away. We have looked into the abyss of infinite content, and the abyss generated a logo for us in four different styles.

So we are not selling you a t-shirt. We are not selling you a poster. Art is useless now. Buy nothing. Make noise instead.
